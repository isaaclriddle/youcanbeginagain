**Early Beginnings**

*You Can Begin Again* was born out of the experiences of a little boy who didn't know he was a boy sitting down to play Obsidian Entertainment's *Fallout: New Vegas* for the first time. My pursuit of masculinity began in my very first player character for the game, who looked nothing like me and was far too confident and charming and masculine to be a true representation of myself in virtual form, yet served for the next *five* years as my muse for my cobbled-together image of the masculine ideal as he was recreated across different games. He began in *New Vegas* as 'Atlas' before shrugging the world off his shoulders and becoming 'Cain' for the world of *Fallout 3,* 'Cato' for a few campaigns of *Dungeons and Dragons* and finally 'Isaac' when I inevitably returned to the Mojave Wasteland.

This archive, like the people whose stories it contains, is both a product of and a response to its surroundings. Created from the intermixing of a beta archive hosted on Scalar and a critical analysis paper discussing Obsidian Entertainment’s *Fallout: New Vegas,* it is a project that should be understood as something iterative.The version that exists now is a response to limitations related to time, labor, and financial support being externally enforced upon it, which is to say that it must adhere to cissexist methods of constructing knowledge environments in order to come into being. In that sense, it also takes inspiration from Dr. K.J Rawson’s [Digital Transgender Archive](https://www.digitaltransgenderarchive.net/) in that this project acknowledges the constraints forced upon it and has resolved to prioritize the “increased open access to trans history” in order to “complicate(s) normative histories” of gender-diverse and trans experiences in video game spaces. 

Future iterations of this archive aspire to draw elements of the embodied presentation of knowledge present in Danielle Braithwaite Shirley’s [Black Trans Archive](https://blacktransarchive.com/), which forces users to reckon both with the humanity of its archival objects through the usage of an avatar creation system. This archive is much earlier in its transition. It has acquired its first prescription – the key to its brand new life existing as it always should have been – yet after administering it, it stares at itself in the mirror wondering why the changes aren’t happening as fast as it hoped they would. It feels itself exist under cis scrutiny and does its best to stand as proudly as it can, donned in shades of pink, white, and blue, but it is still unable to present itself as something outside of the traditional understanding of what an archive ‘should’ be. Please forgive the lumps and bumps and the awkward cracks in its voice; it is still trying to find it.
